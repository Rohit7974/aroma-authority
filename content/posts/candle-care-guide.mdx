Scented candles are more than interior decor—they are complex olfactory engines. To maximize their life and preserve the purity of their fragrance profiles, one must understand the underlying physics and chemistry of wax combustion. 

In this authority guide, we detail how to achieve a clean, soot-free burn from the first light to the last drop.

## The Chemistry of the Melt Pool

When you light a candle, the flame's heat melts the nearby wax. By capillary action, the liquid wax is drawn up the wick into the flame, where it vaporizes and reacts with oxygen to produce light, heat, water vapor, carbon dioxide, and scent molecules.

If the melt pool does not reach the edges of the container, it forms a memory ring. This process, known as **tunneling**, restricts subsequent burns to the narrow center cylinder, leaving unused wax on the walls and drastically shortening the candle's lifespan.

### The Critical First Burn

To avoid tunneling, the first burn is paramount:
1. Allow the candle to burn for **one hour per inch of diameter**.
2. Do not extinguish the flame until the wax melt pool has liquefied completely from edge to edge.
3. For a standard 3-inch jar, this process requires approximately 3 hours of continuous burning.

| Candle Diameter | Estimated Time to Full Melt Pool | Minimum First Burn |
|-----------------|----------------------------------|-------------------|
| 2 inches        | 2 hours                          | 2 hours           |
| 3 inches        | 3 hours                          | 3 hours           |
| 4 inches        | 4 hours                          | 4 hours           |

## Why Wick Trimming Prevents Soot

A long or frayed wick draws liquid wax faster than the flame can efficiently combust it. This imbalance results in incomplete combustion, producing carbon particles (black soot) that coat your walls and containers.

```
Long Wick -> Excess Fuel -> Incomplete Combustion -> Carbon Soot
```

By trimming the wick to **1/4 inch (approx. 6mm)** before *every single burn*, you regulate the rate of fuel delivery. A shorter wick maintains a stable, teardrop-shaped flame and ensures that the organic wax compounds oxidize completely into colorless CO2 and water vapor.

### How to Trim Different Wick Materials
* **Cotton Wicks:** Trim with a dedicated wick trimmer or scissors. Remove the charcoal 'mushroom' cap that forms on top.
* **Wooden Wicks:** Gently pinch off the burnt wood residue using your fingertips or a napkin. Ensure the remaining wood is clean and flat.

## Ambient Drafts and Scent Integrity

Air currents from windows, doors, air conditioners, or ceiling fans introduce excess oxygen into the combustion zone. This makes the flame dance and flicker, cooling the temperature of the reaction.

When the flame cools, the scent throw decreases because the fragrance oils within the wax do not reach their volatilization threshold. Furthermore, flickering causes the flame to melt the container walls unevenly. Always burn candles in a draft-free space.

## Concluding the Burn: Container Safety

When a candle burns to the absolute bottom of the container, the direct heat of the metal wick clip resting on the glass base can cause thermal shock, cracking the container. As a strict safety standard, discard or clean the container when **1/2 inch of wax** remains.

To clean and reuse your luxury jar, pour boiling water into the container to melt and float the remaining wax to the surface. Once cooled, remove the solidified wax disk, wash the glass with warm soapy water, and repurpose it for office desk storage or flower propagation.
